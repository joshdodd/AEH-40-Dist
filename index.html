40 Years Essential

In 1981, five hospitals came together with one common mission: to create a unique voice for hospitals that provide safety-net care. Four decades, three names, and 300 members later, America’s Essential Hospitals remains the nation’s foremost champion for hospitals with a mission to care for underrepresented people and create access to care in underserved communities. This is their story.

▼

1980s THE EARLY DAYS

Larry Gage, Esq., served as deputy assistant secretary for health legislation for U.S. Department of Health, Education, and Welfare—which later split into the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and the U.S. Department of Education—under President Jimmy Carter. In this role, Gage traveled the country cultivating support for legislative proposals among different groups, including state hospital associations, and built relationships with public hospital administrators. He heard their needs—unique in their communities but similar to each other—and became more and more convinced they needed a different type of organization to tell their story.

▼
⬤

Summer 1980

During Gage’s final year in the Carter administration, he invited six public hospital leaders to the office of Rep. Charles Rangel (D-NY).

Rangel knew the group “needed a different pair of eyes to look at the different set of problems that public hospitals had. Their commitment was just as strong as any health provider’s, but their resources were not there,” he says. 

Five of those leaders left Rangel’s office having decided to start a new organization: the National Association of Public Hospitals (NAPH): 

  • Robert (Bob) Johnson, CEO of DC General Hospital;  
  • Richard (Dick) Durbin, from Harris County (Texas) Hospital District;  
  • Sue Brown, from the College of Medicine and Dentistry of New Jersey;  
  • John Sbarbaro, MD, MPH, from Denver General Hospital; and 
  • David Rosenbloom, PhD, former commissioner of health and hospitals for the city of Boston and a founding association member as then-head of Boston City Hospital. 

“They paid $5,000 each in dues and I wished them well,” Gage says. Ten years later, Bob Johnson recalled the payment saying, “The bottom line, of course, is that it turned out to be one of the best investments any of us ever made.”  

That fall, Ronald Reagan won the presidency in a landslide that also took the Senate, and Gage was out of a job—but not for long.

1980 and 1981

The Omnibus Reconciliation Acts created disproportionate share hospital (DSH) payments.

In the Omnibus Reconciliation Act of 1981, Congress gave states greater flexibility in designing Medicaid reimbursement systems, thus breaking the link between Medicare and Medicaid payment methodology. Under this legislation, the Boren Amendment gave states the flexibility to adopt alternative reimbursement methodologies and required states to pay providers amounts that were “reasonable and adequate to meet the costs which must be incurred by efficiently and economically operated facilities.” Initially targeting nursing homes in 1980, the 1981 amendment was expanded to hospitals. These payments became known as disproportionate share hospital (DSH) payments, a vital advocacy issue for America’s Essential Hospitals.

⬤

1982

The association hosted its first annual meeting.

The association announced it was joining the Redbud lawsuit on the steps of Alameda Health System’s Highland Hospital in Oakland, California. Photo courtesy of Rebecca Stanck.
⬤

September 3, 1982

America’s Essential Hospitals successfully inserted the DSH provision into Medicare as part of the Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act of 1982. 

But with no regulations to carry out the law, it was an empty achievement. America’s Essential Hospitals joined other hospital associations in a lawsuit filed by Redbud Memorial Hospital, a small California hospital district that sought a Medicare DSH adjustment because it served a great deal of low-income Medicare patients. The federal district court judge in San Francisco not only ordered relief for Redbud, but also ordered the secretary of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services to implement nationwide regulations. After a series of appeals, the U.S. Supreme Court reversed the lower court’s decision on the nationwide regulations, but upheld Redbud’s right to payment.

⬤

1984

On Capitol Hill, America’s Essential Hospitals worked with supporters, such as Rangel and Rep. Pete Stark (D-CA), to legislate Medicare DSH.

To get around the lack of regulatory support, Stark began writing much more specific language for Medicare DSH, including payment methodology and formulas, that was included in the next budget reconciliation bill. 

 

⬤

1985

With a grant from the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, association staff undertook a study of AIDS care to help uncover the facts of the disease and its treatment and address the surrounding medical, social, and economic questions.

Working with the Council of Teaching Hospitals (COTH), the Institute examined AIDS care across the nation at large, urban public and teaching hospitals—members of America’s Essential Hospitals and COTH. The survey found that the nation’s public and teaching hospitals—and association members in particular—were bearing a disproportionate share of the burden of AIDS care. This association research supported member initiatives, including Harris Health System’s Thomas Street Health Center, the nation’s first freestanding facility dedicated to outpatient HIV/AIDS care, which opened in 1989. The association continues to be a leading force in public health research.

⬤

1987

The National Public Health and Hospital Institute (NPHHI) opened as the association’s research arm.

Since then, Institute researchers have studied a wide variety of issues that affect the health of vulnerable and underserved people. This research gives members a crucial repository of evidence-based studies of the issues readily affecting their patients and their hospitals. Often included in the research are examples of innovative solutions, best practices, and new ideas that can be shared and implemented across the membership and within the larger industry. In addition to informing members’ everyday decision-making, the Institute has informed care and public policy for many leaders and professionals nationwide.

⬤

June 1987

The association published its inaugural issue of The Safety Net, a quarterly news magazine dedicated to sharing information of interest to essential hospitals.

Topics ranged from current health care challenges to legislative updates to recent association and member work. The new communication vehicle demonstrated the growth of the association, which had reached 55 members representing more than 75 hospitals at that point. Years later, the association continues to communicate actively with members, now leveraging email newsletters and social media to share news

⬤

1987

America’s Essential Hospitals established its Fellows Program to help membership develop and inspire their high-potential staff.

The program brings colleagues together to study the current major financing and delivery challenges essential hospitals face and to develop those necessary management and public-facing skills. Many past fellows say the program’s greatest benefit is the access it provides to a network of peers. Many past fellows also have since become association board members and chairs.

1990s COMING INTO OUR OWN

The 1990s were a decade of growth for NAPH. The association had just moved into its first dedicated office in Washington, D.C., and began building its internal staff and infrastructure. NAPH also began laying the groundwork to make essential hospitals’ issues a legislative and regulatory priority.

▼
⬤

January 1990

Chris Burch, a longtime Senate staff member and former Washington representative for association member the New York City Health and Hospitals Corporation (now NYC Health + Hospitals), signed on as executive director.

She remembers those early days as worthwhile but challenging. “All of us did a little bit of everything,’’ Burch said “I signed the checks and I lobbied on the Hill. We didn’t have a surplus or a healthy bottom line.”

Tom Traylor, right, then vice president for federal, state, and local programs at Boston Medical Center, presents King Hillier, King Hillier, vice president of government relations at Harris Health System, with a Safety Net Award in 1998.
⬤

America’s Essential Hospitals created the Safety Net Awards, presented to hospitals for operating innovative programs that successfully meet the health needs of their surrounding communities.

Awards were initially granted in three categories: development, patient care management, and community service. In 2012, the awards were renamed the Gage Awards in honor of retiring association founder Larry Gage. The categories also have changed to reflect current industry events. Categories in 2021 include population health and quality, in addition to a temporary category honoring members’ response to the COVID-19 pandemic. Learn more about the Gage Awards program at https://vital2021.org/gage-awards/.

⬤

1992

Congress enacted the 340B Drug Pricing Program, which required pharmaceutical manufacturers to offer rebates to specific hospitals, including DSH hospitals.

“340B is one of the few federal programs that really gives a direct benefit to the members,” Burch said.

⬤

1993

Members’ engagement around the 340B Drug Pricing Program inspired the creation of the Public Hospital Pharmacy Coalition.

The coalition grew out of America’s Essential Hospitals and eventually broke off to become Safety Net Hospitals for Pharmaceutical Access, renamed 340B Health in 2015. America’s Essential Hospitals continues to work with 340B Health to protect and extend access to this benefit for members.

In the early years, educational and networking opportunities were smaller and geared more toward networking within the membership and on Capitol Hill. Here, 1995 association board chair James W. Wright Jr., meets with Secretary of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Donna Shalala. In a blow to the entire membership, Wright was killed in a plane crash later that year.
⬤

April 10, 1996

Reflecting the changing hospital market, the association added “and Health Systems” to its name, becoming the National Association of Public Hospitals and Health Systems.

⬤

1997

President Clinton signed the Balanced Budget Act (BBA), which posed legislative challenges for essential hospitals.

The BBA broke the longstanding link between Medicaid and welfare eligibility, which added to the difficulty of obtaining coverage and threatened access to care. The BBA also placed a five-year bar on Medicaid coverage for newly arrived legal immigrants and cut state DSH payments for fiscal years (FYs) 1998 through 2002. The DSH cuts were gradual, starting small in the early years and then getting much steeper.

⬤

1997

America’s Essential Hospitals advocacy led to the creation of the State Children’s Health Insurance Program, which insures children in families that cannot afford private insurance but have too much income to qualify for Medicaid.
